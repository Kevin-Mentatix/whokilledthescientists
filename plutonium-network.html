The Plutonium Network

5 companies · 13 scientists · 1 federal decision · filed may 27, 2026
Original research and network mapping by The Leah Files (@leahfiles on X) — deep dive at theleahfiles.substack.com. Additional case research by whokilledthescientists.com.
Click to expand

On May 26, 2026, the Department of Energy quietly announced it would transfer 20 metric tons of weapons-grade plutonium — enough for roughly 2,000 nuclear warheads — to five private companies. The next day, Pam Bondi was added to PCAST, the presidential science advisory board whose members overlap directly with the companies receiving the material.

The Leah Files followed the money through all five companies and found a web of conflicts of interest, revolving doors, and foreign entanglements connecting them to PCAST, the DOE, and each other. Oklo's CEO sits on PCAST while its stock surged 59%. The DOE Secretary sat on Oklo's board until months before taking office. Andreessen invests in Standard Nuclear through a16z — while also sitting on PCAST. One recipient, Exodys Energy, has raised only $2.47M and has no working reactor. Another, Standard Nuclear, is a joint venture with a French state company that has an active partnership with Russia's Rosatom.

Then there are the scientists. 13 researchers connected to nuclear, fusion, and defense programs have turned up dead or missing — and their work traces directly back to the companies and leadership in this network. The most direct link: Nuno Loureiro, who directed the MIT lab that spun out Commonwealth Fusion Systems, was shot dead at home in December 2025. Three months later, Commonwealth Fusion's CEO Bob Mumgaard was named to PCAST. Two months after that, the plutonium deal was announced.

And the lab that sources the plutonium itself — Los Alamos — has lost five connected people since February 2024, including former director Charles McMillan — the man who oversaw the nation’s only plutonium-pit facility and certified the stockpile to the President for seven years — and nuclear-weapon-trigger chemist Philip Leonard. Both were killed in nearly identical head-on crashes on the lab’s own road network, seven months apart.

The Los Alamos Cluster

The lab that sources the plutonium — and keeps losing people

Los Alamos National Laboratory is a primary source of the surplus plutonium being transferred to the five companies above. Since February 2024 the lab has lost five connected people: two killed in head-on crashes on the lab’s own road network within seven months — including its former director — followed by three NNSA-connected workers who vanished within a four-month window in 2025.

THE MAN WHO WOULD HAVE KNOWN
☢
Charles F. McMillan, 69
10th Director, Los Alamos National Laboratory (2011–2017) · Deceased 9/6/24
By multiple measures the most authoritative civilian voice on American nuclear weapons when he died. PhD physics, MIT. 23 years at Lawrence Livermore — W80 warhead project leader, helped establish the Stockpile Stewardship Program — then six years running LANL.

As director he personally oversaw Plutonium Facility 4 (PF-4) — the only facility in America that produces plutonium pits for nuclear warheads — and signed seven annual assessment reports to the President and Congress certifying the stockpile was safe, secure, and reliable.

The oversight didn’t end at retirement. He sat on the Defense Science Board’s Permanent Subcommittee on Nuclear Weapons Surety and was elected by his peers to lead the Nuclear Security Enterprise Integration Council — oversight spanning LANL, Livermore, Sandia, Pantex, and Y-12. His DSB appointment was scheduled to end September 6, 2024 — the day he died. Nine months earlier he had joined Sen. Schumer’s AI & National Security Insight Forum; Sen. Heinrich described being “in deep conversation, mapping out America’s leadership on artificial intelligence” with him shortly before his death.

Killed at ~5:15 AM on NM-502 — the road leading directly to LANL — when a 22-year-old driver crossed the double yellow line and told police she did not remember what led up to the crash. Gouge marks and a witness confirmed the lane departure. A 293-page police report later, the DA found “insufficient evidence” for felony vehicular homicide. April 2026: fully suspended sentence, $1,500 in fines, no jail time.
He knew how much weapons-grade plutonium existed, where it was stored, how it was categorized as “surplus” vs. “required” — and whether any reclassification was legitimate. He died eight months before the executive orders creating the surplus plutonium program were signed. Nuclear Watch New Mexico had called him “the poster child for why the profit motive should not run nuclear weapons facilities.”

☢
Dr. Philip Leonard, 44
High Explosives Synthesis Chemist, LANL
PhD organic chemistry, UC Berkeley. At LANL from 2009 in the high-nitrogen energetics group, later program manager for energetics R&D — the explosive components used in nuclear weapon triggers, one of the most sensitive specialties at the lab. 48 published papers.
He held U.S. Patent 10,421,709 for the production of TATB — the primary insensitive high explosive used in nuclear weapon detonators and explosive lenses. His expertise was the weaponization layer: the capability that makes raw plutonium dangerous.
Killed head-on at ~7:27 AM on NM-501, the LANL truck route, by a 21-year-old driver who crossed the double yellow line after sending two text messages — and who told police she did not remember what led up to the crash. A 199-page police report later: traffic citations only, no felony charges. No LANL press release, no national coverage — Leonard appears on no official “missing scientists” list.
Nuclear weapon trigger design — the exact technology layer between plutonium and a working bomb. Died Feb 27, 2024 — exactly two years to the day before Gen. McCasland vanished (Feb 27, 2026). McMillan was killed on the same road network 7 months later.
